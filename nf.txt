        <h2>Polytheism as a guide to Morality</h2>
        <p><a href="http://drethelin.tumblr.com/post/72908158199/polytheism-as-a-guide-to-morality" class="tumblr_blog">drethelin</a>:</p><blockquote><p>There are a lot of theories of morality, and many of them are fairly compatible in execution if not in theory. Right now I’d like to talk about my intuitive feelings about it, which are not rigorously defined but which help illustrate how I feel somewhat about morality. I’m an atheist and agree broadly with the utilitarian idea that “good” and “bad” can be quantified, but I think the actual process of quantification is something most if not all humans aren’t capable of doing to any reasonable extent. However, this failing does not stop us from needing some sort of guide to what to do with and in our lives. This is where we start to get to Polytheism.</p><p>   To a large extent I approve of virtue ethics, but I think they’re too simplified for the diversity of humanity. Not only do virtues often trade off against each other with no obvious way of deciding which to go with, but different people are inclined emotionally and intellectually to embody different virtues. Thus, instead of striving to embody virtue, I think it might be better to strive to embody gods. Gods are paragons of certain life choices and styles, implying both a search for perfection but also that it’s unattainable. Similarly, they convey that there are certain good things, but that even the best of us will never be ALL of them.</p>
<p>I think humans are very suited to patterning themselves after other humans or human-like paragons. Hence the concept of role models, fashion trends, cults, etc. The entire cultural phenomenon of Rock and Roll involves humans both taking on the role of demigods and striving to pattern their own lives after the people they worship, who simultaneously become both more and less than human to their idolizers. </p>
<p>Polytheism both in the style of god-worship and elevated-human-worship encourages a kind of perpetual becoming/striving towards semi defined ideals. It gives us room for people to strive to be a Hera, a goddess of the hearth and protector of home and safety for family and friends, or to try to become an Einstein or Newton, a dynamo of creation to help all of mankind. A Newton maybe more “important” than a caring protector, but humanity is complex and I want us to have both and more. I WANT us to have our share of worshipers of Bacchus. Even the minions of Loki are a part of the human experience. </p>
<p>Human polytheism/virtuousness doesn’t even need to be embodied in people. Consider the Hogwarts houses. Each is both an eidolon and a system of Virtues. Ignoring the skewed focus of the books, the houses in theory perfectly convey the human need for a variety of moral imperatives suited to different people. What matters is that there exists a social context for virtue that allows people to flow down paths of less resistance. A Slytherin exist in the context of other Slytherins and the virtues of Slytherin, but Slytherin itself lives in the context of Hogwarts, where there is room for many kinds of virtue. </p>
<p>I don’t know what kind of pantheon is best. I certainly don’t believe in the physical existence of gods any more than I believe in br’er rabbit. But I think a morality based on humanistic entities is a big improvement over one based on strict RULES. </p></blockquote>

<p>I think a morality based on humanistic entities might make individuals who choose it happier than individuals who don&#8217;t choose it. But that&#8217;s not the same thing as it being a big improvement. Could you explain why you think that this is better?</p>

        <h2>What &#8220;crazy in public&#8221; feels like from the ingroup</h2>
        <p>I don&#8217;t have an aversive reaction to people stimming, walking weirdly, believing strange things, dressing unusually in public, or talking to themselves out loud in public. Partially because I&#8217;ve interacted with cops every so often for the second, partially because I trained myself out of the last, partially because I see stimming as a thing people do that is totally natural and normal. Also, I hang out around MIT sometimes (OK, a lot)(OK, I&#8217;ve slept there on multiple occasions)(OK, I&#8217;ve taken classes there)(OK, you can find my name on one of the doors).</p><p>All this means that the idea of someone being arrested for being crazy in public never made sense to me. Until last night, when someone presented a vivid example of why people might react negatively.</p><p>I don&#8217;t have a strong attraction to &#8220;normalcy&#8221; in &#8220;the public sphere&#8221;. I think &#8220;the public sphere&#8221; tends to be a bit dull, particularly in England, and I generally want it to be more lively and interesting. So I don&#8217;t get defensive of it, I don&#8217;t feel attached to it, and I don&#8217;t feel like violations of the rules are violations of something that matters to me.</p><p>But I am a lindy hopper. Last night, someone new joined us. He didn&#8217;t dance with anyone, as far as I could tell. Rather, he danced around people, making odd and out of place motions through the floor, barely avoiding hitting people. The first reaction is one of surprise. &#8220;What&#8217;s he doing here? Why isn&#8217;t he acting like everybody else?&#8221; An active dance floor is a fast-paced place, and having someone who isn&#8217;t doing what you expect is scary: in the worst case scenario, someone could get hurt (say, he makes a sudden motion, I move my follow to avoid him, my follow is hit hard by a lead going backwards who thought that I was going to continue on path). </p><p>Later on, when everybody was standing in a circle and clapping on beat for the dancers in the center, he was making large motions and extraneous noise that disrupted the intent, focused attention on himself, and continued to <b>not do what everyone else was clearly doing</b>. It made the event less than it could have been.</p><p>This provoked a disgust reaction in me. Why? Because we were all here, doing a thing, building energy and community and being together, and then here comes this dude tearing it down. If you want to be with us, please be willing to adopt local norms. When you draw attention to yourself like that, it distracts from why we all want to be here. This isn&#8217;t an unreasonable request, right?</p><p>Thankfully, I had the cached thought &#8220;they&#8217;re not harming anyone, being crazy in public should be acceptable.&#8221; I ended up being the first person to talk with him, to try to welcome him in. But I had to override my anger, fear, and disgust reactions to do that.</p><p>People aren&#8217;t mean because they want to hate others. &#8220;Please respect our cultural norms, even if they&#8217;re unwritten&#8221; seems like a reasonable request. Overcoming this may require active ability to suppress negative reactions.</p><p>At the same time, there&#8217;s an important second point to make. If he hadn&#8217;t been clearly neuroatypical (he put his shirt into his mouth for extended periods) the response would have been gentle correction from one of the more experienced dancers. An inability to tease apart what isn&#8217;t going to change (sucking on his own clothing, making large movements that distract from the energy of the moment) from what might (moving through a full dance floor) led to nothing being said. It seemed like people pre-emptively abandoned normal attempts at socialization through small conversations about etiquette in favor of tornado protocol. I don&#8217;t know that this is optimal, though I am inclined to defer.</p><p>It would be nice to be able to add at the end &#8220;and then we had a fascinating conversation about computational game theory and the state of academia.&#8221; But that would be a lie, and besides, empathy and niceness don&#8217;t need to be justified by external rewards. The endless stories about how &#8220;I was nice to someone, and then everything became wonderful&#8221; have never appealed to me. I don&#8217;t reach out to people because they will give me nice things: I reach out to people because it&#8217;s right, and because I want to be the sort of person who reaches out to the lonely and the sick.</p>

<p>I’m afraid that overriding “kudos” so its pluralization is “kudos” is going to break things and confuse Rails</p></blockquote>

<p>Okay, so obviously once this question had been brought up I couldn’t rest until I got to the bottom of it. &lt;/language-lawyer&gt;</p><p>What you want is for “kudos” to be treated as an uncountable noun, like “data”. You can tell Rails to do that by adding this to config/initializers/inflections.rb:</p><pre><code>ActiveSupport::Inflector.inflections(:en) do |inflect|
  inflect.uncountable 'kudos'
end</code></pre>
<p>From there, you can use “kudos” in pretty much any context, singular or plural, and it will work.</p><p>There is one caveat: If you have kudos declared as a resource in config/routes.rb (i.e., <code>resources :kudos</code>), then the route for the index of all kudos will be named <code>kudos_index</code> (it can’t be named <code>kudos</code> because that name is used for the route for an individual kudos). <a href="http://markembling.info/2011/06/uncountable-nouns-rails-3-resource-routing">This article</a> explains it all.</p></blockquote>

<p>I’ve never actually used Rails and basically any time anyone talks about it in any kind of depth near me my primary reaction is “holy shit that is way too fucking much magic”</p></blockquote>

<p>Heh. “Magic” is a compliment among Ruby programmers.</p><p>I find it interesting how so much of the difference among code in different programming languages is attributable to community norms rather than actual language features. For the most part, you can do things like the aforementioned pluralization engine, or otherwise use metaprogramming features and runtime hacks to make the language into something that scarcely resembles its original form, in Python just as much as in Ruby. The two languages are very similar in terms of what features they offer. The difference is that Ruby programmers will be like “wow, that’s useful, isn’t it great how much flexibility Ruby gives you?” and Python programmers will be like “OH GOD WHY WOULD YOU DO THAT”.</p></blockquote>

<p>Yes. This. I&#8217;m proud that I can do an immense number of stupid things in python. I&#8217;m not saying it&#8217;s ever a good idea to take advantage of the fact that the variable of a for loop will leak in Python, but I&#8217;ve done it, and commented on what I was doing and why. I would guess that in Ruby it&#8217;s also entirely feasible for an intermediate programmer to make variables that change upon access and the like. Every once in a long while, you might do one of these things, if it was the only way to solve a problem.</p><p>Cleverness is good! Cleverness is impressive! Cleverness makes debugging much much harder and should be treated as a last resort, not as a first solution for when you can&#8217;t name your variable what you want to.</p>

        <p><b><a href="http://funereal-disease.tumblr.com/">funereal-disease</a> asked: Opinion meme: cultural relativism!</b></p><p><a href="http://loki-zen.tumblr.com/post/112225172695/opinion-meme-cultural-relativism" class="tumblr_blog">loki-zen</a>:</p><blockquote><p><a href="http://loki-zen.tumblr.com/post/112224599295/opinion-meme-cultural-relativism" class="tumblr_blog">loki-zen</a>:</p><blockquote><p><a href="http://dataandphilosophy.tumblr.com/post/112090227286/opinion-meme-cultural-relativism" class="tumblr_blog">dataandphilosophy</a>:</p><blockquote><p><a href="http://loki-zen.tumblr.com/post/112083940795/opinion-meme-cultural-relativism" class="tumblr_blog">loki-zen</a>:</p><blockquote><p><a class="tumblr_blog" href="http://dataandphilosophy.tumblr.com/post/112073571821/opinion-meme-cultural-relativism">dataandphilosophy</a>:</p>

<blockquote><p>Two alternative definitions: the practical and the ethical (Wikipedia says it’s only what I call the practical meaning, but that’s not how people I know tend to use it in practice, and it’s more boring.) The practical is what Wikipedia gives, and it’s interesting: the claim that the only/most effective way to understand someone is within the context of their own culture. Their beliefs and activities can only be understood within the framework of their culture.</p><p>The way that this is wrong is that culture should be understood as “the X tribe” but rather as a series of non-concentric circles (actually shapes in concept-space but circles are easier to imagine so pretend I’m taking the intersection with a particular plane), some of which contain each other. The culture of “Tumblr LW” is part of the culture of “LW diaspora” is mostly part of the culture of “educated western elites” is part of the culture of “Westerners” is part of the culture of “Moderns” is part of the culture of “humans”. I can understand Gilgamesh’s sadness over the death of Enkidu because we share the culture “humans”. Maybe I don’t understand it all that well, because we have different ideas about what is good and what death is, but we agree that death is bad and that immortality would be good. </p><p>The culture of “Oxford” is part of both the culture of “college towns” (think music in the streets, lots of book stores, lots of young people, lots of clubs, lots of late night food providers, lots of poetry readings, plenty of cultural and artistic institutions) and the culture of “England” which is part of the culture of “The United Kingdom” which is part of the culture of “Westerners” and you know the rest. Because I came from the culture of “Cambridge, MA” which is also part of the culture of “college towns”, there are parts of Oxford that I understand well, and parts that I don’t understand as well. </p><p>Talking about cultural relativism makes it easy to deny the cultural similarities that do exist, the concepts that do overlap, and the analogies we can draw. I do share a culture with people with very different backgrounds, and there are plenty of things in common.</p><p>However, the way that cultural relativism is right is that it is really easy to draw false analogies. Brains are great at connecting new things to a pattern, thereby making false universals. If I try to understand Augustine’s views on sexual morality while acting like he’s a utilitarian, I’m going to have a bad time. This is related to the difference between steelmanning and being charitable. Cultural relativism emphasizes charity, and I think that that is great.</p><p>——————————————————————————————</p><p>On the ethical side, I’ve always had a question of “why shouldn’t I impose my values on others?” If the response is “because X”, cultural relativists are trying to sneak in a different value system, and should be ignored, and if there is no response, then I am willing to accept that different cultures have different value systems, and that I shouldn’t judge someone as good or bad except by their own value system (much the same standard that Brandon Sanderson and I apply to books, I will add), and I’m fine with that. I’m a utilitarian: I was never fond of judging people as good or bad anyways. I just want to maximize utility.</p></blockquote><p>This is a good post and I agree, but the example of Cambridge in the UK is a bit off? The concept of ‘College Town’ is not a British concept (primarily because pretty much every city and big town in the UK has at least one University, so it isn’t really a specific thing).</p><p>The culture of Oxford goes Oxford’s culture &gt; Oxbridge locations (since Oxford and Cambridge as universities have a lot of cultural institutions unique among universities that still exist in the modern day) &gt; wealthy historical cities in the UK (London, Edinburgh, York etc) &gt; southern English culture &gt; UK culture &gt; Europe &gt;Western etc.</p><p>It goes straight from southern English to UK because in terms of difference in culture space ‘southern England’, ‘northern England’, ‘Midlands’, ‘Scotland’ and ‘Wales’ are all pretty much equally distinct from each other, so an ‘English’ subset is not really a natural category.</p></blockquote><p>Thank you! This ignorance is one of the many misconceptions that come from spending time in Oxford, London, and no place else in all of the UK. Hopefully this will be rectified this break. Thank you for that lesson! While I know that the British consider Northern v Southern to be a big deal, I wasn’t aware that it was Northern v Scottish had similar weight.</p><p>I was using Cambridge, MA to mean Cambridge, Massachusetts, USA. Of course Cambridge, UK and Oxford, UK  are extremely similar. But I find it interesting that Cambridge, MA and Oxford, UK have similarities that US Cambridge doesn’t have with New York and that UK Oxford doesn’t have with London. </p></blockquote>

<p>I mean, I meant Cambridge UK, but it is true that there are things more in common between Oxford and US-Cambridge there - from what I hear, some guy deliberately named the place Cambridge in the hope someone would build an analogous university there, so that’s one thing. But also (though I haven’t been to Cambridge MA) I get the impression that they are both places where one feels like the big thing about the town is the University - in other words, the place is small enough that the University-flavour is very strong in the general flavour of the town. (In fact, I’d be inclined to argue that Oxford and Cambridge are pretty much the only two places in the UK that *do* feel like college towns, because the size and fame of their universities is out of proportion to the size and fame of the town in a way that is usually reversed in the UK)</p><p>Whereas New York and London both have tons of universities and similar institutions, but are also big enough and have enough other shit going on that University-flavour is pretty dilute in the final tasting. They also have things in common like ‘big international tourist attraction city’, ‘big financial centre city’, ‘high immigrant and minority population city’ and ‘depicted a bazillion different ways in various forms of media city’. And they’re both really really big to the point where they don’t even feel like a single place a lot of the time, and they’re places where people are often more attached identity-wise to the district in which they live rather than the city as a whole.</p><p>Anyways, I love talking about this crap and I hope you have a good time when you visit!</p><p>The North-South divide is also interesting because it’s a lot more pronounced when you look at cities/towns other than the very biggest. So a lot of Manchester looks like London looks like Edinburgh looks like Birmingham because yeah. But go just a little bit smaller, and you’ll see how Oxford or Bristol or Norwich have red brick, and cobblestone, and deliberately preserved little tea shops, Tudor-looking buildings and various other twee things, as well as rivers or canals clearly adapted for pleasure use. </p><p>Meanwhile Huddersfield or Halifax or Doncaster is just plain a different shape and colour - they’re full of soot-blacked stone, the *old* buildings are taller and squarer (they’re industrial), the river or canal may be adapted for pleasure but shows much stronger signs of trade use, and there’s hardly a Tudor-looking building to be seen outside of York or Harrogate. If the town isn’t a tourist trap the old buildings are being used in a non-twee/touristy manner (lots of offices and flats in old mill buildings) and the town centre is visibly less wealthy.</p><p>In addition, pretty much every UK city is characterised by a mish-mash of old and new - take a panoramic shot of London and get everything from the Roman wall to a skyscraper they haven’t finished building yet in one frame - but if a town has taken potentially costly steps to preserve it’s ‘look’ and thus is more uniformly old-looking, 80% chance it’s in the South. Which if you ask me is a combination of they can afford it and that there’s a different cultural aesthetic at play.</p><p>My hypothesis that this is to do with Northern pragmatism vs Southern aestheticism is played out when you then come across a Northern city with better economic incentives to keep it’s old-building vibe - say, York, aka Foreign Touristville, Yorkshire - and find that it is an exception to the rule.</p><p>A recent thing (which, sadly, this being Tumblr, I am not going to cite) even pointed out that, despite how much we move around, there remain genetic differences between Northerners and Southerners as groups. The North has much more Viking and Celt heritage (except, where Celtic is concerned, Wales and Cornwall) while the South has more Norman and Anglo-Saxon, and this is a smooth gradient thing, not an abrupt change at Hadrian’s Wall. Wherever you are in the UK, there *is* a National Trust property near you.</p><p>* Go to the theatre. If you are in a big city and it is expensive, it is likely to be good. If you are in a tiny town/city and it is cheap, it will either be amazing and great value for money, or hilariously tacky and low rent in a way that is pretty much worth it in and of itself.</p><p>* If you are from the US, eat chocolate/take home chocolate. You guys do not get decent chocolate at home.</p><p>There was totally more but I forgot</p></blockquote>

<p>Oh I think some of the ‘more’ was that if it is urban, it is absolutely feasible to get there on public transport.         <p><b><a href="http://daughter-of-adam.tumblr.com/">daughter-of-adam</a> asked: Unpopular opinion meme: Wild animal suffering is a bigger problem than the suffering of animals in factory farms</b></p><p>Lacking better evidence, I&#8217;m going to say that I would predict evolutionary processes to favor enough happiness to make things want to survive if they can think about the question, and if something doesn&#8217;t want to survive, then I&#8217;m not convinced I should care about it. <p>This is not merely flirting, this is the ideal form of non-productive conversation.</p></blockquote>

<p>What do you mean by non-productive conversation? Giving and recieving infodumps sounds very productive to me, particularly if I’m asking questions and really understanding what the other person is talking about.</p></blockquote>

<p>By non-productive, I mean “for the purposes of having fun, not trying to accomplish a specific task”. Info-dumping may be productive for some specific tasks, but lots of goal-oriented conversation (working together on a project, trying to get someone to like you, trying to catch the person you’re talking to lying) can occur more effective formats.</p><p>I’m not saying mutual infodumping is inherently unproductive, just that it’s the clear winner in fun-oriented scenarios.</p></blockquote>

<p>So you’re distinguishing between “good at accomplishing a particular task” and “good in general”, and only calling the former productive. It’s a different definition than what I would use, but I can work with it.<br/><br/>Also, why do you think that mutual infodumping is better than arguing about something? It’s talking about something you’re passionate about that the other person is also passionate about, and you get to see how their mind works, and you get to engage with the person. I think of all of these as improvements.</p></blockquote>

<p>Yes, arguing is really nice too, and I forgot to think of it when calling infodumping the best. I think I sounded too serious and confident in my first reblog, I was trying to state that mutual infodumping is really fun in non-romantic contexts and should be used more often, and I made too strong a statement. I thought my intent would be clear, but clearly it wasn’t, because you can only see the words I write, not all the context I have in my head. Curse you, illusion of transparency!</p><p>Also, I’m not trying to define “productive”, I just wanted some word meaning “trying-to-accomplish-a-non-immediate-fun-goal” without sounding too negative and “productive” came to mind.</p></blockquote>

<p>I admit, &#8220;ideal&#8221; made me think &#8220;but X is better than Y so Y can&#8217;t be ideal&#8221;, and I&#8217;m trying to cultivate the virtue of accepting that different people think very differently from me, so I thought it plausible that you did prefer it as a form of flirting (and casual conversation.) I agree that mutual info-dumping is really fun in non-romantic contexts (until you realize she asked you about metaphysics and was thinking of &#8220;para-psychology&#8221; when you meant &#8220;neo-platonism&#8221;. Info-dumping is much more fun when both people agree about what info is.)</p><p>Ok.</p>
